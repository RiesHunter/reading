# Sample Selection
 - This section seems to be about sampling for surveillance versus investigation.

## 3.1 Representative sampling
 - "[S]pecimens are selected for sequencing such that the pathogen genetic diversity in the sequenced sample set is representative of the pathogen genetic diversity circulating in the broader population."
 - Frequencies should match.
 - Be careful of sampling bias -- e.g., severe disease, higher pathogen concentration in samples, host tropism of a pathogen. (Watch out for what you're over-including and under-including.)

Questions for representative sampling (from the text):
1. What is the frequency of this variant in my population?
2. How are frequencies of these different variants changing over time?
3. When was this particular pathogen genotype introduced to the population, and how long has it circulated for?
4. How much pathogen diversity do we observe in our community, and how does this relate to pathogen diversity in other communities?

## 3.2 Targeted sampling
 - "[W]e aim to sequence as many samples as possible from a particular population, outbreak, or transmission chain, in order to understand the specific genotypes and disease dynamics associated with that population or setting."

## 3.3 Contextual data
 - To supplement targeted sampling, you often want some background, representative samples. These are called _contextual data_.

### 3.3.1 Contextual data as a backdrop
 - "[D]escribes what is happening more broadly outside your densely sequenced target population."
 - Can better define the spatio-temporal bounds of an outbreak.
 - Useful for molecular-clock calculations.

### 3.3.2 Contextual data as controls
 - If you are investigating whether there's an association between genotype and clinical presentation, you need to include contextual data as controls. If your targeted population (with severe disease, for example) is not genotypically diverse, you still can't conclude that the genotype is associated with severe disease. Only with proper controls can you support this distinction. You must look at samples that did not have the clinical presentation of interest.
 - "Similarly, representatively-sampled contextual sequences act as controls in genomic epidemiological analyses. They allow you to see which pathogen genotypes circulate in individuals _not_ presenting with the altered clinical course. Much like the case where everyone at the picnic ate the potato salad, it is completely possible that a particular genotype is dominantly circulating in a community and causing infections with varied clinical presentations. If this is the case, then your targeted samples may all be infected with the same genotype, but your representatively-sampled contextual sequences will also show that same genotype."
