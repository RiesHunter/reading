# Chapter 1: The Value of Pathogen Genomics in Applied Epidemiology

## 1.1 The value of genomic epidemiology for surveillance
 - Epi curves w/o genomic data may erroneously suggest a single introduction event when there are actually "distinct introduction events of divergent genotypes." This distinction can be made with genomic epidemiology.
 - "Genomic data provide additional resolution to determine relationships between cases."

Benefits of Genomic epidemiology
 1) can "detect the emergence or introduction of new lineages"
 2) can "distinguish introduction events from endemic transmission within the surveillance area"
 3) can show "how different clusters contribute to the overall disease burden"
 4) "enables public health authorities to intervene in more precise and tailored ways"
 5) can investigate whether a variant's increase in incidence is associated w/fitness or an event

 - "Like standard surveillance data, genomic data are observational data, and therefore to infer causality between genotype and presentation you would need to draw upon other experimental approaches. 

## 1.2 The value of genomic epidemiology for outbreak response
 - "Pathogen genome sequences can help us detect or rule out linkages between cases."
 - Example: each location has a mixture of genotypes, suggesting we did not observe location-specific introductions. Instead, the mixing is likely due to travel following a community-aquired infection. 
 - Example: one location has a mixture of genotypes; the other has one genotype. Without genomic data, we would "believe that all of these cases are related" and may have acquired the infection at the location. With genomic data, it is clear that the location is a major factor in transmission risk. "This would allow us to target our intervention efforts toward this group of workers."
 - Improves case definitions, too. If one genotype is known to carry a higher risk of severe disease, we can intervene accordingly. This is valuable clinically and epidemiologically.

# 1.3 The value of retrospective data
 - "can increase our understanding of the dynamics and timing of outbreaks."
 - Earlier detection of a specific genotype might suggest undersampling.
 - "Though this information isn't actionable for any response happening now, it does nonetheless paint a much clearer picture of when these viruses emerged, and puts constraints on hypotheses about past events."
 - May be most useful in resource-limited settings.
 - Findings are expected to confirm transmission we hypothesized was occurring.
